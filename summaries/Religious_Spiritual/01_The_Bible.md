<!-- High-Level Theological Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 60px;">
    <h3 style="color: #000; font-size: 2rem; margin-bottom: 25px; font-weight: 300;">The Dialectic of Justice and Mercy</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 40px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.15rem; color: #1a1a1a;">
        The narrative tension of the Bible lies in the character of a God who is both perfectly just and limitlessly merciful. From the primitive wanderings of the patriarchs to the sophisticated Greco-Roman world of the New Testament, the text relentlessly pursues the "Imago Dei"—the idea that humanity bears the divine image. 
        <br><br>
        The New Testament introduces a radical disruption: the Incarnation. It proposes that the infinite has entered the finite, not as a conqueror, but as a sacrifice. This subversion of power dynamics—where the first shall be last and the weak shall be strong—has sparked more social and spiritual revolutions than any other document in human history. It remains the ultimate text for those seeking the intersection of the temporal and the eternal.
    </div>
