<!-- High-Level Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 60px;">
    <h3 style="color: #fff; font-size: 1.8rem; margin-bottom: 25px;">The Hard Science of Hope</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 40px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1rem; color: #dcdcdc;">
        Andy Weir’s debut is a love letter to human ingenuity. Unlike many Sci-Fi novels that rely on "magic" tech, <em>The Martian</em> is meticulously grounded in real-world physics, chemistry, and orbital mechanics. The narrative tension is derived entirely from Watney’s problem-solving process—turning the book into a high-stakes engineering puzzle. 
        <br><br>
        Beyond the science, it explores the global psychological impact of a singular human life. The "Watney Event" unites Earth in a way that politics cannot. It is a story that proves that the human spirit, when filtered through the lens of logic and persistence, can withstand even the most hostile environments in the solar system.
    </div>
