    <h3 style="color: #1a1a1a; font-size: 2rem; margin-bottom: 25px; font-weight: 900;">The Mask of Professionalism</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 50px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.1rem; color: #333; opacity: 0.9;">
        The brilliance of <em>The Hating Game</em> lies in its character contrast. Lucy is short, colorful, and pathologically nice, while Joshua is tall, monochrome, and pathologically precise. Thorne uses their office dynamic as a theatre where they perform their mutual dislike to hide their burgeoning vulnerability. 
        <br><br>
        The "Elevator Scene"—a cornerstone of modern romance—serves as the breaking point where the performative hate yields to an undeniable physical and emotional pull. Thorne avoids the clichés of "insta-love" by grounding their attraction in years of careful observation. They know each other’s habits, coffee orders, and weaknesses better than any friend ever could. It is a story that proves that sometimes, the person you "hate" the most is the only person who actually sees you.
