    <h3 style="color: #63b3ed; border-bottom: 1px solid #2d3748;">&lt;KEY_CONCEPTS /&gt;</h3>
    <ul style="list-style-type: '=> ';">
        <li style="margin-bottom: 10px;"><strong>The Broken Window Theory:</strong> Fix "bad code" (broken windows) immediately to prevent the whole system from rotting.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom: 10px;"><strong>Orthogonality:</strong> Ensure that your modules are independent. Changing one should not break others.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom: 10px;"><strong>Prototype to Learn:</strong> Use throwaway code to understand a problem before implementing the final solution.</li>
        <li style="margin-bottom: 10px;"><strong>Design by Contract:</strong> Specify exactly what a component expects and what it promises to deliver.</li>
    </ul>

    <h3 style="color: #63b3ed; margin-top: 0;">// SUMMARY_INDEX.MD</h3>
    <p><em>The Pragmatic Programmer</em> is widely considered the most influential book on software development. It doesn't focus on a specific language but on the mindset and techniques required to be a "craftsman." Thomas and Hunt introduce iconic concepts like <strong>Don't Repeat Yourself (DRY)</strong> and <strong>Rubber Ducking</strong>. It covers everything from project management to social communication and technical excellence. The book teaches that programming is a lifelong journey of learning, where the best tool in your kit is your own critical thinking.</p>
