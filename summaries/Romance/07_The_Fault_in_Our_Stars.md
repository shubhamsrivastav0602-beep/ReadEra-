<!-- High-Level Existential Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 70px; position: relative; z-index: 1;">
    <h3 style="color: #ffffff; font-size: 1.8rem; margin-bottom: 25px; font-weight: 400; font-style: italic;">The Oblivion of the Self</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 50px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.1rem; color: #e1e8ed; opacity: 0.8;">
        Augustus Waters’ primary fear is "oblivion"—the idea that his life might end without having made a "mark" on the world. Hazel, conversely, views herself as a "grenade" that will eventually destroy the lives of those she loves. Their romance is a collision of these two philosophies, ultimately concluding that a meaningful life is not measured by its length or its legacy, but by its intensity. 
        <br><br>
        John Green uses the book-within-a-book, <em>An Imperial Affliction</em>, to explore the frustration of narratives that end mid-sentence. He argues that life is messy, unfair, and often concludes without resolution. <em>The Fault in Our Stars</em> is a heartbreaking and darkly humorous reminder that the only thing we truly own is the present moment—and that love is the only worthy use of that time.
    </div>
