<!-- High-Level Literary Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 70px; position: relative; z-index: 1;">
    <h3 style="color: #3e2723; font-size: 2rem; margin-bottom: 30px; font-weight: 200; font-style: italic;">The Realism of the Soil</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 50px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.15rem; color: #5d4037; opacity: 0.9;">
        Premchand’s genius lies in his "Idealistic Realism." He doesn't just show the poverty; he shows the dignity of the poor. Through the parallel narrative of the urban intellectual world and the rural farm world, he exposes the hypocrisy of the ruling classes. Hori’s struggle for the "Godan" (the gift of a cow) is a metaphor for the unattainable dreams of millions.
        <br><br>
        The characters of **Dhania** (Hori’s resilient wife) and **Gobar** (his rebellious son) represent the two sides of India—traditional endurance and the upcoming call for change. <em>Godan</em> is a mandatory read, offering a visceral and high-fidelity roadmap of the Indian heart. It is a work of absolute authenticity that remain unmatched in world literature.
    </div>
