<!-- High-Level Satirical Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 60px; position: relative; z-index: 1;">
    <h3 style="color: #1a1a1a; font-size: 1.8rem; margin-bottom: 25px; border-bottom: 1px solid #eee;">The Logic of the Lawless</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 50px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.05rem; color: #333; opacity: 0.9;">
        Shukla’s brilliance lies in his "dry" humor—a wit that is both laugh-out-loud funny and deeply tragic. He dismantles the "idealistic gaon" myth, showing it to be a place of petty rivalries, caste wars, and absolute apathy. The transition from idealism to cynicism in the protagonist Rangnath represents the disillusionment of an entire generation. 
        <br><br>
        The novel’s most high-fidelity achievement is its language—a mixture of scholarly Hindi and rural slang that captures the very soul of North Indian power-play. <em>Raag Darbari</em> is a mandatory read, offering a cynical yet accurate roadmap of the Indian machinery. It is a work of absolute intellectual integrity that remain one of the sharpest critiques of democracy ever written in Hindi.
