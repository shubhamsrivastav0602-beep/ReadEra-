<!-- High-Level Philosophical Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 70px; position: relative; z-index: 1;">
    <h3 style="color: #1a1a1a; font-size: 2rem; margin-bottom: 30px; font-weight: 200; font-style: italic;">The Will to Meaning</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 50px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.1rem; color: #1a1a1a; opacity: 0.9;">
        Frankl’s core theory—**Logotherapy**—holds that our primary drive in life is not pleasure, as Freud believed, but the discovery and pursuit of what we personally find meaningful. He illustrates this with the stark reality of the camps: those who had a task waiting for them, a loved one to return to, or a vision of the future were the ones who survived against impossible odds. 
        <br><br>
        The book is divided into two parts: a visceral account of the horrors of the concentration camps, and a technical explanation of Logotherapy. But the bridge between them is the profound realization that man’s ultimate freedom is his "Inner Freedom"—which cannot be taken by any external power. <em>Man’s Search for Meaning</em> is one of the most intellectually and spiritually rigorous books ever written. It is a mandatory rigorous training for any human being, providing a map for the soul when the world falls into darkness.
