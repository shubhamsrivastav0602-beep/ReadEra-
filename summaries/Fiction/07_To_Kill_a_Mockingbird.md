<!-- High-Level Literary Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 70px; position: relative; z-index: 1;">
    <h3 style="color: #3e2723; font-size: 2rem; margin-bottom: 30px; font-weight: 200; font-style: italic;">The Death of the Mockingbird</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 50px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.15rem; color: #5d4037; opacity: 0.9;">
        Lee’s prose is warm, rhythmic, and deceptively simple. Through the eyes of a child, she dismantles the complex layers of racial and class-based prejudice. The novel’s most powerful theme is the "Mockingbird"—a symbol for the innocent who are destroyed by the evil of others. Both Boo Radley (the recluse) and Tom Robinson (the accused) are mockingbirds in their own right. 
        <br><br>
        The figure of **Atticus Finch** stands as one of literature's most enduring icons of moral integrity—a man who understands that "courage is not a man with a gun in his hand," but knowing you're licked before you begin but you begin anyway and see it through no matter what. <em>To Kill a Mockingbird</em> is a mandatory read, offering a visceral and high-fidelity roadmap of the American conscience. It is a work of absolute beauty and sorrow.
