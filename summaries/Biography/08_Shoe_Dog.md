<!-- High-Level Biographical Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 70px; position: relative; z-index: 1;">
    <h3 style="color: #1a1a1a; font-size: 2rem; margin-bottom: 25px; font-weight: 900;">The Art of the Float</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 50px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.1rem; color: #333; opacity: 0.9;">
        Knight’s narrative is surprisingly literate and self-deprecating. He explores the "Zen" of entrepreneurship—the ability to stay calm while being perpetually broke and in debt to Japanese suppliers. He highlights the "Buttfaces"—the core team of misfits and rebels who helped him build Nike. 
        <br><br>
        The book handles the evolution of a brand from a product to a culture. Knight argues that Nike was never about just shoes; it was about the spirit of the athlete and the refusal to lose. <em>Shoe Dog</em> is a mandatory read, providing a masterclass in grit, high-fidelity branding, and the absolute necessity of following a "crazy idea." It is a testament to the fact that the hardest part of the journey is simply not stopping.
