<!-- High-Level Psychological Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 60px;">
    <h3 style="color: #1a1a1a; font-size: 1.8rem; margin-bottom: 25px;">The Terror of the Threshold</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 40px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.05rem; color: #333;">
        Holden's obsession with "phoniness" is a defense mechanism against the complexity of maturity. He idealizes his deceased brother Allie and his younger sister Phoebe because they represent a purity that he feels is being eroded in himself. His weekend-long odyssey through New York is a series of failed connections, each one reinforcing his sense of isolation. 
        <br><br>
        The central metaphor—the "Catcher in the Rye"—is Holden's misguided desire to stop time and protect children from the fall into adulthood. Salinger uses a stream-of-consciousness style that feels uncomfortably intimate, forcing the reader to confront their own transition from innocence to experience. The novel’s resolution—with Holden in a sanitarium—remains a powerful commentary on the price of authenticity in a world that demands conformity.
    </div>
