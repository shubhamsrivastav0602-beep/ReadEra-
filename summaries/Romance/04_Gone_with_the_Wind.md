<!-- High-Level Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 60px;">
    <h3 style="color: #1a1a1a; font-size: 1.8rem; margin-bottom: 25px;">The Paradox of Scarlett O'Hara</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 40px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.05rem; color: #34495e;">
        Scarlett is a protagonist of infinite complexity. She is selfish, manipulative, and often heartless, yet her sheer grit in the face of starvation and siege is undeniable. Mitchell’s genius lies in stripping away the romanticism of the South, replacing it with the cold reality of a woman who turns her back on "lady-like" virtues to save her ancestral home, Tara. 
        <br><br>
        Her relationship with Rhett Butler is a psychological chess match. Rhett, a man of profound cynicism and hidden vulnerability, is the only one who truly recognizes Scarlett’s nature. Their romance is not about "softness," but about the collision of two forces of nature. The novel’s unresolved ending is a masterpiece of realism—a reminder that even the strongest can lose everything, yet still find the strength to face tomorrow.
    </div>
