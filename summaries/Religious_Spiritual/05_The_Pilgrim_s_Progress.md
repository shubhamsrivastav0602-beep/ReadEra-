<!-- High-Level Allegorical Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 60px; position: relative; z-index: 1;">
    <h3 style="color: #3e2723; font-size: 1.8rem; margin-bottom: 25px;">The Psychological Geography of Faith</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 40px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.05rem; color: #5d4037;">
        Bunyan’s genius lies in naming his characters after the abstract forces they represent: Obstinate, Pliable, Mr. Worldly Wiseman, and the Giant Despair. Each encounter is a externalization of a spiritual conflict. The "Burden" on the protagonist Christian’s back is his sin, which only falls away when he reaches the Cross—not through effort, but through grace.
        <br><br>
        The narrative remains a mandatory text for understanding the "Puritan Mindset," yet its themes of perseverance, temptation, and hope are universal. It teaches that the spiritual life is not a static state, but a dynamic, often dangerous "progress" towards a higher reality. Bunyan’s plain-spoken yet powerful English helped shape the literary style of the King James Bible era and continues to resonate as a profound guide to the human soul.
    </div>
