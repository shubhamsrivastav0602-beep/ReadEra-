    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 40px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.05rem; color: #ccc;">
        Through Winston Smith, Orwell dissects the slow disintegration of the human spirit. Surveillance via the 'telescreen' is only the surface; the deeper horror is the manipulation of memory. By controlling the past, the Party controls the future. Winston’s brief rebellion—falling in love with Julia—is a desperate attempt to reclaim a private, biological reality in a world where every heartbeat is a political act.
        <br><br>
        The climax in Room 101 is the ultimate realization of totalitarian power. It is not enough for the state to destroy its enemies; it must convert them. The chilling resolution is not Winston's death, but his psychological surrender. <em>1984</em> remains a mandatory warning against the erosion of truth and the fragility of liberty.
