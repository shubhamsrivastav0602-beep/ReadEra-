<!-- High-Level Philosophical Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 60px;">
    <h3 style="color: #1a2e35; font-size: 1.8rem; margin-bottom: 25px;">The Anatomy of Obsession</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 40px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.05rem; color: #1a2e35;">
        Melville uses the <em>Pequod</em>—a ship with a crew from every corner of the globe—as a microcosm of humanity. While Ishmael observes with a detached, almost scientific curiosity, Ahab is consumed by a singular, metaphysical hatred. To Ahab, Moby Dick is not a whale, but a "pasteboard mask" for a cruel deity. 
        <br><br>
        The prose is biblical in its power and encyclopedic in its depth. Melville pauses the action to discuss the history of whaling, the biology of leviathans, and the philosophy of "The Whiteness of the Whale"—a chapter that explores how the absence of color can be more terrifying than any monster. The novel is a visceral reminder that the price of conquering nature is often the destruction of the self.
    </div>
</div>
