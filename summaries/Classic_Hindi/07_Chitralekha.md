<div style="margin-bottom: 70px; position: relative; z-index: 1;">
    <h3 style="color: #3e2723; font-size: 2rem; margin-bottom: 30px; font-weight: 200; font-style: italic;">The Relativity of Virtue</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 50px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.15rem; color: #5d4037; opacity: 0.9;">
        Verma’s masterpiece is a visceral, high-fidelity interrogation of morality. He argues that "sin" is not a fixed universal entity, but a Result of circumstances and human choices. Chitralekha, the beautiful courtesan who captures the hearts of both the lover and the monk, serves as the catalyst for their transformation. 
        <br><br>
        The novel’s conclusion is revolutionary—it suggests that the "saint" may be full of pride and ego (sin), while the "sinner" may be capable of ultimate sacrifice and love (virtue). <em>Chitralekha</em> is a mandatory read, offering a philosophical roadmap that challenges every preconceived notion of morality. It is a work of absolute intellectual bravery that remain as relevant today as it was in 1934.
