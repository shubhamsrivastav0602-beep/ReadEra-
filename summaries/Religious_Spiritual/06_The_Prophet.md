<!-- High-Level Philosophical Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 70px; position: relative; z-index: 1;">
    <h3 style="color: #1a1a1a; font-size: 2rem; margin-bottom: 30px; font-weight: 200; font-style: italic;">The Wisdom of the Intermediate</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 50px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.15rem; color: #4a4a4a; opacity: 0.9;">
        Gibran’s brilliance lies in his ability to articulate the "intermediate" truths of existence. He doesn't advocate for asceticism or hedonism, but for a balanced, soulful engagement with the world. On marriage, he speaks of the pillars of a temple that stand apart; on work, he describes it as "love made visible." 
        <br><br>
        The book operates on a frequency of deep empathy and cosmic perspective. It suggests that our joy and our sorrow are inseparable—the deeper that sorrow carves into our being, the more joy we can contain. <em>The Prophet</em> is a mandatory rigorous training for any soul seeking to align their daily life with the rhythms of the infinite. It is a work of absolute beauty that transcends religion and speaks to the universal human heart.
    </div>
