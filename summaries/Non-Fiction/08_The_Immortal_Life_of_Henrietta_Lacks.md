<!-- High-Level Philosophical Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 60px; position: relative; z-index: 1;">
    <h3 style="color: #1a1a1a; font-size: 1.8rem; margin-bottom: 25px;">The Commodity of the Core</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 40px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1rem; color: #2d3436;">
        Skloot masterfully balances the clinical history of HeLa with the deeply personal story of the Lacks family. She explores the "Informed Consent" crisis—or lack thereof—that characterized 1950s medicine, particularly regarding Black patients. The narrative tension lies in the contrast between the pristine world of the laboratory and the poverty-stricken reality of Henrietta’s descendants. 
        <br><br>
        The book asks profound questions about bodily autonomy. Do you own your cells once they leave your body? Who should profit from biological discoveries? Through the character of **Deborah Lacks** (Henrietta’s daughter), we see the emotional toll of knowing your mother is "immortal" in a jar but gone from your life. <em>Henrietta Lacks</em> is a mandatory read, serving as a powerful intersection of science, race, and the fundamental right to be remembered as a human being, not just a specimen.
    </div>
