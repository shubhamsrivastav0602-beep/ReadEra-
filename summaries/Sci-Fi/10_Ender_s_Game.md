    <h3 style="color: #fff; font-size: 1.8rem; margin-bottom: 25px; border-bottom: 1px solid #1f252d;">The Ethics of the Simulation</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 50px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1rem; color: #a9b7c6; opacity: 0.9;">
        The central brilliance of <em>Ender's Game</em> is the subversion of the "Chosen One" trope. Card explores the psychological cost of child soldiers and the absolute isolation of genius. Ender is forced to win so decisively that his enemies never dare strike again, a strategy that saves the world but destroys the child. 
        <br><br>
        The novel also delves into the tragedy of Xenocide—the unintentional destruction of a species due to a failure in communication. Ender realizes too late that the Buggers were not attacking, but trying to connect. This revelation transforms the thriller into a profound meditation on empathy. <em>Ender's Game</em> is a mandatory rigorous training for any leader, providing a visceral lesson on the burden of power and the absolute necessity of understanding the 'Other.'
    </div>
