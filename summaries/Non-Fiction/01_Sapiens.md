<div style="text-align: justify; background: #fafafa; padding: 20px; border-radius: 5px;">
    <h3 style="color: #3498db; margin-top: 0;">📝 FULL SUMMARY</h3>
    <p>Yuval Noah Harari’s <em>Sapiens</em> is a sweeping narrative that traces our species' journey from obscure African apes to the masters of the planet. The core argument is provocative: Sapiens achieved dominance not because of superior brains or strength, but because of their unique ability to believe in "imagined realities." These include concepts like money, laws, and human rights—none of which exist in the physical world but allow millions of strangers to cooperate effectively.</p>
    
    <p>The book breaks human history into four major phases: The Cognitive Revolution (the birth of language), the Agricultural Revolution (the shift from hunting to farming), the Unification of Humankind (the creation of global systems), and the Scientific Revolution (the era of data and industry). Harari doesn't just present facts; he challenges the reader to question whether this "progress" has actually made us happier or if we are simply more powerful "gods" who don't know what we want.</p>
</div>

<div style="margin-top: 30px; border: 1px solid #e74c3c; padding: 15px; border-radius: 5px; background-color: #fdf2f2;">
    <h3 style="color: #e74c3c; margin-top: 0;">⚠️ CRITIQUE (SKEPTIC'S NOTE)</h3>
    <p>Critics often argue that Harari oversimplifies complex historical trends and that some of his biological claims are more speculative than scientific. The book is highly deterministic and sometimes overlooks individual agency in favor of massive historical shifts.</p>
