<!-- High-Level Literary Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 70px; position: relative; z-index: 1;">
    <h3 style="color: #1a1a1a; font-size: 2rem; margin-bottom: 25px; font-weight: 300; border-bottom: 1px solid #eee;">The Architecture of the Ego</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 50px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.1rem; color: #444; opacity: 0.9;">
        Agyeya’s brilliance lies in his application of European modernist techniques (like stream of consciousness) to the Indian context. Shekhar is a "rebel" not just against British rule, but against every fossilized social norm. The relationship between Shekhar and **Shashi** is the novel’s emotional and intellectual core—a bond that transcends traditional morality and delves into the nature of shared consciousness. 
        <br><br>
        The novel is visceral, sharp, and high-fidelity. It demands that the reader confront their own internal contradictions. <em>Shekhar: Ek Jivani</em> is a mandatory read, offering a roadmap for the evolution of the modern Indian intellectual. It is a work of absolute intellectual integrity that redefined what the Hindi novel could be.
