<!-- The Moral Architecture Deep Dive -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 60px;">
    <h3 style="color: #1a1a1a; font-size: 1.8rem; margin-bottom: 25px;">// THE ARCHITECTURE OF MAYCOMB</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 40px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.05rem; color: #34495e;">
        Scout Finch is perhaps the most crucial "witness" in American literature. Her innocence is not a lack of intelligence, but a lack of bias—a clean mirror held up to a distorted society. Atticus Finch, the unwavering moral anchor, represents the radical idea that integrity is not a performance but a biological necessity. 
        <br><br>
        The narrative arc follows the transition from the whimsical "Boo Radley" adventures of childhood to the crushing weight of the courtroom. The trial of Tom Robinson is the fire that tempers Scout and Jem’s character, teaching them that while the truth may not always win in a court of men, it is the only thing that allows one to live with oneself.
    </div>
</div>
