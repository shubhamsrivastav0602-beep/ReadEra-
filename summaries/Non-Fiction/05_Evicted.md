<!-- High-Level Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 60px;">
    <h3 style="color: #1a1a1a; font-size: 1.8rem; margin-bottom: 25px;">The Commodity of Shelter</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 40px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1rem; color: #333;">
        Desmond dismantles the myth that the poor are simply irresponsible. He shows a system where rent consumes 70-80% of an income, leaving families just one unexpected bill away from losing their roof. Through characters like Arleen and Lamar, we see how the "Mark of Eviction" makes it impossible to find decent housing, forcing the most vulnerable into substandard, dangerous conditions.
        <br><br>
        The book is also a study of the landlords—Sherrena and Tobin—who are not presented as cartoon villains, but as actors in a broken market. Desmond argues that the U.S. has made housing a commodity rather than a right, and that the only way to break the cycle is through systemic policy change and a universal housing voucher program.
    </div>
