<!-- High-Level Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 60px; position: relative; z-index: 1;">
    <h3 style="color: #2d3436; font-size: 1.8rem; margin-bottom: 25px;">The Ethics of the Self</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 40px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.05rem; color: #4a4a4a;">
        The narrative tension of <em>Me Before You</em> lies in the conflict between Louisa’s desperate hope to save Will and Will’s firm conviction to end his life on his own terms. Moyes handles the controversial subject of assisted suicide with a rare delicacy, forcing the reader to confront the question: What does it truly mean to respect someone's autonomy? 
        <br><br>
        As Louisa expands her world through Will’s intellectual guidance, Will rediscoveries a sense of joy through Louisa’s unbridled vitality. Their romance is not a "cure," but a "catalyst." It is a heartbreaking reminder that sometimes, loving someone means letting them go, even if it leaves an irreparable hole in your own life. The novel’s legacy is its insistence on living a life that is "insanely great," no matter how much time you have.
    </div>
