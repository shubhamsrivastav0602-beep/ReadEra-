    <h3 style="color: #1a1a1a; font-size: 1.8rem; margin-bottom: 25px; border-bottom: 1px solid #eee;">The Advantage of Local Knowledge</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 50px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.1rem; color: #333;">
        Lynch’s primary philosophy is that "Wall Street is the last to know." He encourages investors to look for companies that are "boring," "ugly," or "unloved" by the big institutions. Lynch categorizes stocks into six types—slow growers, stalwarts, fast growers, cyclicals, turnarounds, and asset plays—and provides a specific strategy for each. 
        <br><br>
        He also warns against "diworseification"—the tendency of great companies to buy mediocre ones. The book is written with a dry wit and a refreshing lack of jargon, making it as entertaining as it is educational. <em>One Up on Wall Street</em> is a visceral reminder that the stock market is not a casino, but a gallery of businesses. If you understand the business, you understand the stock. It is a mandatory rigorous training for any aspiring retail capitalist.
    </div>
