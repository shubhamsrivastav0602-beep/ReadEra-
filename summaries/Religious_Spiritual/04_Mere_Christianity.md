<!-- High-Level Theological Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 60px;">
    <h3 style="color: #1a1a1a; font-size: 1.8rem; margin-bottom: 25px; border-bottom: 1px solid #eee;">The Law of Human Nature</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 40px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.1rem; color: #34495e;">
        Lewis begins not with the Bible, but with a universal human experience: our intrinsic sense of Right and Wrong. He calls this the **Moral Law**. He argues that because even those who deny morality still complain when they are treated unfairly, there must be an objective standard outside of us. 
        <br><br>
        He further explores the "Great Sin"—Pride. While most people view pride as a minor character flaw, Lewis identifies it as the "ultimate evil," the state of mind that competes with God. The book culminates in the idea that Christianity is not about being "good," but about being "new." It is the process of turning a biological creature into a spiritual one through a deliberate "re-centering" of the soul.
    </div>
