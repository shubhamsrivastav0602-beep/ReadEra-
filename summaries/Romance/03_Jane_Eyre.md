<!-- Deep Dive: The Psychic Connection -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 60px;">
    <h3 style="color: #2c3e50; font-size: 1.8rem; margin-bottom: 25px;">The Anatomy of a Soul Bond</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 40px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.05rem; color: #34495e;">
        What separates Jane and Rochester from typical romantic leads is their intellectual parity. They do not communicate in pleasantries, but in challenges. Rochester, a man haunted by a devastating marital secret, finds in Jane a person he cannot buy or bully. Her morality is her armor, and it is this very armor that ultimately saves him.
        <br><br>
        The novel's climax—the discovery of Bertha Mason in the attic—is more than a plot twist; it is a test of Jane's autonomy. In fleeing Thornfield, she chooses herself over her desire. Her eventual return is not an act of surrender, but a choice made as an "independent will." Brontë’s writing remains a haunting exploration of self-respect.
    </div>
