<!-- High-Level Theological Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 60px;">
    <h3 style="color: #4e342e; font-size: 1.8rem; margin-bottom: 25px;">// THE SYMPHONY OF THREE PATHS</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 40px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1rem; color: #5d4037;">
        Krishna introduces Arjuna to a layered reality, where the battlefield of Kurukshetra is a metaphor for the internal war of desires and duties. He proposes three distinct technologies for the soul: <strong>Karma Yoga</strong> (The path of selfless action), <strong>Jnana Yoga</strong> (The path of analytical knowledge), and <strong>Bhakti Yoga</strong> (The path of devotional surrender).
        <br><br>
        The genius of the Gita lies in its refusal to be dogmatic. It acknowledges that every individual has a unique disposition (Svabhava) and thus requires a unique path. Krishna does not demand blind obedience; he demands critical inquiry and the eventual realization that the individual self is an inseparable wave in the ocean of cosmic consciousness (Brahman).
    </div>
