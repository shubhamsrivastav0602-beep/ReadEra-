<!-- High-Class Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 60px;">
    <h3 style="color: #ff6b6b; font-size: 1.8rem; margin-bottom: 25px; font-family: 'Baskerville', serif;">The Architecture of Preconception</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 1; column-gap: 30px; text-align: justify; color: #2c3e50; font-size: 1.1rem;">
        <p>
            Elizabeth Bennet represents the emergence of the modern protagonist—intellectually agile, morally steadfast, yet humanly flawed. Her journey is not one of finding a husband, but of dismantling the "prejudice" that shields her from the vulnerability of love. Opposite her stands Fitzwilliam Darcy, a man whose "pride" is a fortress built of duty and social standing. Their collision at the Meryton Ball is the spark that ignites one of literature's most complex psychological battles.
        </p>
        <p style="margin-top: 20px;">
            Austen uses the Bennet household as a microcosm of class struggle. While Mrs. Bennet represents the desperate materialism of the era, and Mr. Bennet the cynical detachment, Elizabeth finds a path between them—a path of genuine emotional intellectualism. The resolution is not merely a marriage certificate, but a radical transformation of two souls who learn to look past the surface of things.
        </p>
    </div>
</div>
