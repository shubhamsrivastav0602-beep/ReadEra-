<!-- High-Level Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 60px;">
    <h3 style="color: #1a1a1a; font-size: 1.8rem; margin-bottom: 25px;">The Fracture of Belonging</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 40px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1rem; color: #34495e;">
        Westover's prose is as sharp and precise as the scrap metal she once salvaged. She examines the gaslighting and abuse of her childhood not with bitterness, but with a forensic curiosity. The central tragedy of the memoir is that to "save" her mind, she had to "lose" her family. 
        <br><br>
        Her education acts as both a weapon and a shield. As she gains the vocabulary to describe her trauma, she also gains the distance that makes reconciliation impossible. <em>Educated</em> is a visceral reminder that the most difficult thing we can do is to change our own minds about where—and to whom—we belong.
    </div>
</div>
