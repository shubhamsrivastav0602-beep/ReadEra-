<!-- High-Level Philosophical Analysis -->
<div style="margin-bottom: 60px; position: relative; z-index: 1;">
    <h3 style="color: #1a1a1a; font-size: 1.8rem; margin-bottom: 25px;">The Search for the Infinite</h3>
    <div style="column-count: 2; column-gap: 40px; text-align: justify; font-size: 1.05rem; color: #333;">
        Through the character of **Pierre Bezukhov**, Tolstoy explores the agonizing search for moral purpose in a world of bloodshed and vanity. Opposite him is **Andrei Bolkonsky**, who seeks glory only to find it hollow under the vast sky of Austerlitz. The novel is a visceral reminder that the "small" moments of life—a ball, a hunt, a birth—are where the true substance of existence resides. 
        <br><br>
        Tolstoy famously concludes the novel with a lengthy philosophical essay on history, arguing that it is the collective, unconscious willpower of millions—not the commands of Napoleon or Alexander—that drives human events. To read <em>War and Peace</em> is to undergo a transformation, to see the interconnectedness of all living things and to realize that the struggle for meaning is the only struggle that matters.
