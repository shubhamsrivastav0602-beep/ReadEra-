## 🎯 ALL ANIMALS ARE EQUAL. BUT SOME ANIMALS ARE MORE EQUAL THAN OTHERS.

### 📖 One-Line Hook:
*"On a farm where the animals overthrow their human master, a utopia of equality devolves into a tyranny even worse than before—until the pigs and the humans become indistinguishable."*

George Orwell's 1945 political allegory is a devastating satire of the Soviet Union and a timeless warning about the corruption of revolutions. Using farm animals to represent the figures of the Russian Revolution, Orwell shows how idealism is corrupted, how power corrupts, and how the powerful always rewrite history.

This is not just a book about politics. It is a book about human nature—and the eternal tendency of those who seize power to become exactly what they overthrew.

## 📖 STORY ARC: The Revolution Betrayed

### 🌿 THE SETUP: "Mr. Jones, of Manor Farm, had locked the henouses"

Old Major, the prize-winning boar, gathers the animals for a secret meeting. He inspires them with a vision: one day, the animals will overthrow the humans and the farm will be theirs. "All animals are equal."

Weeks later, Major dies. The animals rise up—surprisingly easily—and drive Mr. Jones from the farm. They rename it Animal Farm. They write the Seven Commandments on the barn wall. The highest principle: "Whatever goes upon two legs is an enemy."

The early days are utopian. The animals work together, share the harvest, sing "Beasts of England." The farm thrives.

---

### ⚖️ THE CONFLICT: "Napoleon is always right"

Two pigs emerge as leaders: Snowball and Napoleon. They have different visions—Snowball wants to improve the farm; Napoleon wants to rule it.

In one of the novel's most pivotal moments, Napoleon releases his nine puppies—a private army he has been raising. They attack Snowball, driving him from the farm. From that moment, Napoleon is the sole leader.

The dogs do not merely give orders; they enforce them. Any animal who questions is punished. The confessions come—animals who admit to conspiring with Snowball are torn apart.

---

### 🌅 THE CORRUPTION: "Some animals are more equal than others"

The novel tracks the gradual corruption of Animal Farm.

The pigs move into the farmhouse. They begin drinking. They walk on two legs. The commandments are quietly altered:

"ALL ANIMALS ARE EQUAL, BUT SOME ANIMALS ARE MORE EQUAL THAN OTHERS."

The pigs trade with humans. They become indistinguishable from the farmers they replaced. Napoleon himself lives in the house, drinks whisky, sleeps in beds.

The novel's final horror: the pigs invite farmers to see the farm. They are indistinguishable from the humans—so much so that when the farmers look, "the creatures outside looked from pig to man, and from man to pig, and from pig to man again; but already it was impossible to say which was which."

## 🎭 MAIN CHARACTERS: Personalities

### Napoleon (⚡)

Napoleon is the revolutionary turned tyrant. He is sly, brutal, and patient. He doesn't argue with Snowball—he simply seizes power through his private army. Once in control, he becomes exactly what he overthrew.

### Snowball (⚡)

Snowball is the idealist—the pig who wants to improve the farm, bring electricity, modernize. He is a true believer in Animalism. But he is no match for Napoleon's ruthlessness.

### Boxer (⚡)

Boxer is the workhorse—the massive horse who does the farm's hardest labor. His "Napoleon is always right" is both touching and tragic. When he is no longer useful, Napoleon sells him to a glue factory.

### Squealer (⚡)

Squealer is the propagandist—the pig who explains away every contradiction, reinterprets every principle. He is the voice of the Party.

### Old Major (⚡)

Old Major is the visionary—the pig who inspires the revolution with his dream. He dies before it begins, and his legacy is quickly corrupted.

## 📝 FULL SUMMARY (SPOILER-FREE FIRST 150 WORDS)

Animal Farm is Old Major's dream—a utopia where animals rule themselves, free from human exploitation. When the animals overthrow their lazy farmer Mr. Jones, the revolution seems successful.

But the pigs—who are the smartest animals—take control. Snowball wants to build a windmill to bring electricity to the farm. Napoleon wants to focus on food production. They argue endlessly.

Then Napoleon activates his secret weapon: the nine puppies he has been secretly raising. They attack Snowball, driving him from the farm. From now on, Napoleon is the sole leader.

The dogs enforce his rule. Any animal who questions is accused of conspiracy and torn apart. The confessions come daily. Animals who admit to plotting with Snowball are executed.

The pigs move into the farmhouse. They begin drinking alcohol. They walk on two legs.

The Seven Commandments are quietly altered. The most important principle is changed: "All animals are equal, but some animals are more equal than others."

The farm trades with humans. The pigs sit around a table with farmers, drinking. They are indistinguishable—so much so that when the farmers look at the pigs' meeting, "the creatures outside looked from pig to man, and from man to pig, and from pig to man again; but already it was impossible to say which was which."

Boxer—the massive workhorse who believed in the farm—is sold to a glue factory when he is no longer useful. He is "sent to the knacker," and the pigs drink alcohol to celebrate his departure.

Animal Farm has come full circle: the revolution has been betrayed. The pigs are the new farmers. The other animals are no better off than before—perhaps worse.

The only change is that there is no hope of escape.

## ⭐ READER'S VERDICT: Why You Should Read

Animal Farm is not just a great political novel—it is a timeless warning. Orwell wrote it in 1945, directly after World War II, as a warning about how revolutions become tyrannies.

Read it for the allegory—the parallels to Soviet Russia are precise and devastating. Read it for the prose—Orwell writes with clarity that makes the horror feel inevitable. Read it for the warning—the novel has been applied to every revolution since, proving it was right.

This is one of the most influential novels of the twentieth century—sold in millions, read in schools, studied in every political science class.
